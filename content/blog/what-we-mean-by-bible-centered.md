On our site and in almost every conversation, you'll hear us describe Arise as a "Spirit-filled, Bible-centered" church. We've written before about the first half of that phrase. I want to be just as plain about the second, because "Bible-centered" can land in very different ways depending on what you've lived through. For some it sounds like solid ground. For others it sounds rigid, or like the opening line of an argument you'd rather not have. If that's you, stay with me, because what we mean is probably gentler, and far more hopeful, than you'd expect.

## Start with what the Bible actually is

Before it's a set of rules or a subject to debate, the Bible is how God has chosen to speak. We believe the Scriptures are his word, trustworthy and true, given not to burden us but to lead us to life. They're not a collection of ancient opinions we're free to take or leave, and they're not a self-help manual with God's name stamped on the cover. They're God telling us who he is, what he's done, and how we were made to live.

That's why we don't just *include* the Bible at Arise, alongside a dozen other voices. We center on it. When it speaks, we listen, even when it corrects us, especially when it corrects us. A church can drift toward whatever's loudest in the culture or whatever the pastor happens to feel strongly about that week. The Bible is the fixed point that keeps us honest, and keeps us pointed at Jesus instead of at ourselves.

## Not a rulebook, and not a weapon

Here's the part I most want you to hear, because I know some of you have been on the wrong end of a Bible verse. Being Bible-centered does not mean we treat Scripture as a rulebook to measure people with, or a weapon to win arguments. The Bible has been misused that way, and it does real damage. That is not what we're about.

Jesus himself said the Scriptures point to him (John 5:39). So the goal was never to master a book, it was to meet a Person through it. When we open the Bible together, we're not hunting for ammunition or a checklist. We're listening for the voice of the God who loves us and wants to set us free. Truth and grace came together in Jesus, and they belong together in how we handle his word.

## What it looks like on an ordinary Sunday

On a normal Sunday, being Bible-centered is pretty undramatic. We open the Scriptures and actually read them. We try to understand what they meant before we ask what they mean for us. We let the text set the agenda instead of using it to decorate an agenda we already had. And then we ask the harder question: not just "what does this say," but "what does this ask of me?"

You don't need a theology degree or a lifetime of church to be part of that. You just need to be willing to look. Some of the best questions we've heard came from people who'd never opened a Bible before, or who'd walked away from one years ago. There's room for all of it here, the doubts, the pushback, the "wait, does it really say that?" Honest wrestling with the text is welcome, it's how many of us got here.

## Why this matters to us

We're planted in partnership with The Foursquare Church, and we share its historic conviction that the Bible is the inspired, trustworthy word of God, the rule for what we believe and how we live. We hold that gladly, and we don't apologize for it. But we hold it the way you'd hold something precious that's meant to be given away, not clutched to win a fight.

A Bible-centered church isn't one that argues the most about Scripture. It's one that lets Scripture actually change it, our pride softened, our fears quieted, our lives slowly reshaped into the likeness of Jesus. That's the whole point. The word does its work on us, not just through us.
